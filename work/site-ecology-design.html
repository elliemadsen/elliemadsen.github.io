site | ecology | design

coursework at RISD's masters in landscape architecture program

SITE | ECOLOGY | DESIGN is a RISD architecture studio in which students engage with issues of remediation through the hands-on study of an urban waterfront shaped by millennia of natural processes and centuries of human interaction.

The coastline of the Narragansett Bay and Seekonk River have been drastically transformed in the past centuries since the marshes were filled in, altering the coastal ecology in service of local shipping and manufacturing industries. Today, the Rhode Island Department of Environmental Management classifies the Seekonk as an impaired waterway.

Sitting on the east bank of the Seekonk River in Providence, Rhode Island, the Phillipsdale Historic District is a mill village that spans over 80 acres of land. Occupying the southwest corner of the district is Phillipsdale Landing, a vacant 25-acre plot of abandoned asphalt, concrete and steel infrastructure slowly giving way to early successional species. Decades after a series of shipping, manufacturing, and forgery companies, approximately 75% of the site is classified as a combination of disturbed fill and impervious surfaces.

Despite the land's apparent deterioration, it maintains an extraordinary sense of place and distinct visual identity. Many industrial artifacts remain intact, creating a constellation of past memorabilia among pockets of regrowth, patches of restoration. I wrote an essay on this tactile experience for The Forager, a student environmental literary magazine: Pre-Colonial Forging, Post-Industrial Foraging: Seeking Stories of Production on the Seekonk.

"Foraging the remains of a demolished steel forgery, I can't help but play with these words in my mind: forage, forge. Forage. Forge.

Forge: to make or shape.

Forage: to search or obtain.

They read as stark opposites, yet in a different light they are exactly the same.

The word connecting them both, I believe, is assemble. To forage is to assemble is to forge, and vice versa."

The studio environment gave me opportunity to extend the theories and themes I'd learned in the classroom to the specific context of the Phillipsdale Landing site, while exploring the relationships between human/nature, foraging/forging, stewardship/ownership, tradition/technology, de/composition, and de/colonization across a variety of mediums: found objects, projection drawings, 3D models, photography, writing, and digital renderings.

"This land has long been home to narratives of creation: from today's industrial factories, tangled in the global web of capital, to yesterday's local economy of Naragansett artisanship. But even long before this land was touched by human hands, it was still a hub of production. The lifecycle of an ecosystem necessarily involves bringing objects into existence. Compounds assemble to create organisms anew; life forms amass to form symbioses. Human supply chains and ecological food chains apply the same principles of cyclic creation across vastly different beings and scales. After all, a tree and a factory are both plants."

For my final site plan I designed a minimally invasive proposal to encourage natural succession and regeneration through the creation of more-than-human habitat, biodiverse native planting, and regenerative agriculture.

My core design principles were decolonization, ecological succession, urban agriculture, indigenous knowledge, and more-than-human cohabitation.

Ecological Systems and Habitat Zones

Inspired by Tuck & Yang's seminal work, decolonization is not a metaphor, an integral aspect of my site plan was repatriation of the land to its original stewards, under whose care it previously flourished: the Naragansett people.

The proposal leaves most of the site's surface area undeveloped and includes limited human-oriented programming on the uncontaminated north-east region of the site. This programming centers around community gardens with plots available to local renters who lack access to private land, in an effort to increase access to green space and reduce local food insecurity. Additional interventions include a pavilion, seed library, composting facility, walking path, orchard, retention ponds, and nesting platforms.

Clay Contour Models

I designed terraced gardens to work with the land's existing slope, increase water conservation, and avoid digging into the nutrient-depleted soil. Community gardens would create a safe and accessible public space to practice growing methods indigenous to the Narragansett and Wampanoag regions, such as companion planting, intercropping, polycultures.

Sections

Lining the 800-foot path from the gardens down to the shore of Omega Pond are a series of retention ponds for irrigation. The walking path ends with a boardwalk overlooking Omega Pond. The brackish water of the Seekonk presents a suitable climate for microalgae farming, which could be used as carbon sequestration, biomass fuel, or nitrogen-additive for composting.

The space would be co-operated by community organizations such as the Narragansett Food Sovereignty Initiative, Tomaquag Museum, Zero Waste Providence, Groundwork Rhode Island, and the Southside Community Land Trust. Individual garden plots would be free to rent for East Providence residents.

Elevations

A remediation strategy that balances hands-off ecological succession with light-touch human intervention can transform the site a productive landscape once more. This transition signifies a departure from the site's extractive industrial legacy, and a return to its natural ecosystem.
