monk house

architecture design

In an Architecture Design Studio course at Brown, I was tasked with designing a home for a Buddhist monk on a 20x20' plot of land in Providence, RI.

I identified several key aspects of monastic life to guide my design principles: material simplicity, modesty, minimalism, domestic labor, privacy, sustainability, and connection nature.

The four-level spatial plan segments the space into functional tiers. At the lowest level, an underground cellar and woodshed represent the most labor-intensive aspects of monastic life. The next level houses most daily activities and includes a bathroom, kitchen, and wood-burning stove. It is partially inset underground for insulation. The ground floor serves as a meditation and ritual space, symbolizing a physical and metaphorical elevation above the material needs of daily life. Windows placed above eye level allow natural light while maintaining privacy. This floor has direct access to a Japanese garden and stone meditation labyrinth. Finally, the upper loft is a place for sleep, the most transcendent of habits. Suited for a humble floor mat, it is directly above the wood-burning stove for optimal heating.

To minimize the resident’s environmental footprint and encourage rituals of labor, the home’s roof includes a vegetable garden and rain collection system.

The structure is composed of a locally sourced timber frame and straw bale infill, providing natural insulation and serving as a substrate for an exterior layer of plaster. These substances adapt traditional Buddhist temple materials to a New England climate, while the refined modern exterior accommodates the neighborhood’s visual language.
