Enter asciiworld


asciiworld is an interactive web environment that explores themes of worldbuilding, hypertext, gaming nature, digital gardening, and information poorness. The visual abstraction of the rendered environment works as a metaphor for the whimsical and subversive user experience.

From 7 bits arise a discrete set of 128 characters, which give way to infinite combinations of graphical representation. Up close we read each character as a discrete element: #, $, %. Zooming out, the figure becomes an effect emerging from the field itself: a moment of intensity, a peak or valley.

ASCII art flattens the character; letters are not to be read by humans as we are taught, nor by machines as they are trained. The characters are stripped of meaning, semiotically void. Their affordances lie in their visual density - the ratio of positive to negative space, ink to paper — allowing for alternate readings: character as text, as image, as hyperlink, as word, as world.

Sonya Rapoport, a pioneer of computer art, describes her work as “an aesthetic response triggered by scientific data.” She uses computers to make “tracings of her personal vocabulary icons, developing themes and stories with words.” In Donna Haraway's Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction, she contrasts the traditional linear hero's journey—the spear—with the historically feminine narrative taking the shape of a bundle, a container, a bag for gathering. The world I have created is one manifestation of such: characters not written by humans as logical strings nor read by machines as binary sequences, but symbols that are gathered, planted, shared, and jumbled. Haraway states: "Perhaps it is precisely in the realm of play, outside the dictates of teleology, settled categories, and function, that serious worldliness and recuperation become possible."

My ASCII world lives inside the carrier bag. Worlds are weaved from a cosmos of binary decisions. We exist in an era of hi-definition computer graphics, hyper-resolute virtual realities, and attempts to render digital experiences in the image of our mind's eye, to be experienced as one experiences the physical world. There is a reactionary urge to turn back to the handmade web, low tech interventions, information poorness, data sparsity. I inch towards the threshold at which an image, rendered computationally, might disintegrate into abstraction. In the gaps left behind by the absence of vivid renderings, popup ads, and infinite scrolls, there is space. I intend to pry this space open: to let it grow, expand, and inside, to search for its poetic capacity.
