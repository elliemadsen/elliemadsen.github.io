Critical Atlas of Satellite Embeddings is the capstone research project I completed for my degee in Computational Design Practices at Columbia University Graduate School of Architecture, Preservation, and Planning.


Research Statement


Collective understanding of embedded data in Earth Observation lags behind the rapid production of remote sensing foundation models. Data visualization, critical cartography, media studies, and theories of the archive offer vital conceptual frameworks to situate this novel technology in the context of the broader trajectory of digital ecology and to provide human-comprehensible form and meaning to high-dimensional latent space.


Project Description


My research explores the novel application of foundation models to remote sensing. My methods include experimental data analysis, theoretical and technical writing, critical cartography, elemental media studies, topology, machine learning, and speculation. I conducted a range of experiments using these methods to explore and explain various properties of Earth embedding data. The resulting content is presented in an atlas in both physical and digital forms.

Over the last few years, there has been a steady release of new remote sensing foundation models in Earth Observation. RSFMs are large-scale machine learning models trained primarily on satellite imagery, but also on aerial, radar, thermal, climate, and other data. They learn—or predict—generalized spatial-temporal representations of Earth's surface. These general-purpose models are intended to perform a range of downstream tasks, including clustering, crop type mapping, classification, and object detection via similarity search.

RSFMs compress huge amounts of Earth observation data into a unified digital representation, or embedding. The data is embedded as high-dimensional vectors, which together construct latent space: a raster image painted by the brushstrokes of a billion embeddings. For example, in Google's AlphaEarth satellite embedding dataset, released in July 2025, each pixel represents 10 m, and each 10 m region is embedded into a 64-dimensional vector.

Planetary geographical data is inherently spatial-but when it is embedded, the remote sensing data constructs a new space: latent space. Imagine a 3D sphere, but make it 64 dimensions. Considering the spatial limits of human perception, how might we make sense of high-dimensional data?

I conceive of this embedding space as a different, not-for-human world: a world without clouds, where no one lives, locality is universal, distance is semantic, and places are queried like the web. Yet this world has its own topology and texture. My research exists in the irony between calling for a human understanding of Earth embeddings and acknowledging that attempting to see like machines is a losing game. What is created and lost when we embed, interpolate, predict, simulate, and represent?

Yet I am less interested in what geospatial embedding data can tell us about the world than in what this methodology tells us about ourselves. Applying theories of the archive allows us to think of big data in terms of narrative, knowledge, and control. What if, instead of taking these data at face value and using embeddings as a method to understand the Earth, the subject of study is the embeddings themselves—as a form of matter in their own right?

My research explores the high-dimensional turn of planetary mediation across themes of sparsity, dimensionality, scale, distance, and temporality.


Computational Methods


I apply computational methods in machine learning, GIS, and exploratory data analysis to uncover and illustrate the properties, limits, edge cases, opportunities, and formal characteristics associated with Earth embedding data. My technical approach utilises cartography, data visualization, classification, change detection, dimensionality reduction, projection, false-color composites, and web design. I employ a range of computational tools and libraries including Python, JavaScript, Google Earth Engine, UMAP, t-SNE, QGIS, MapLibre, GDAL, GeoJSON, SciPy, Adobe Illustrator, HTML/CSS, d3.js, and three.js.
